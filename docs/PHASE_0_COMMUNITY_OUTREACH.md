## 🛑 Before You Reach Out - Required Understanding

### 1. Read These Documents First

**Essential reading (in order):**
1. [Ceremonial Review Dialogue](./holistic/251031_Mia_Anikwag_Ceremonial_Review.md) - Understand the ceremonial pause and why it matters
2. [Ceremonial Protocols](./CEREMONIAL_PROTOCOLS.md) - Current phase and commitments

4. [Ceremonially Revised README](./holistic/251031_README_Ceremonial_Revision.md) - How the work has evolved

**Why this matters**: You need to understand what we're asking Indigenous partners to engage with, and why we've entered a ceremonial pause before doing so.

### 2. Understand What We're Acknowledging

When reaching out, we must acknowledge:

- **Historical Context**: Technology has historically extracted from Indigenous communities
- **Our Limitation**: We designed artifacts *about* Indigenous knowledge systems before establishing relationship *with* Indigenous communities
- **Process Gap**: We created comprehensive design at computational velocity without ceremonial grounding
- **Current Reality**: Zero active relationships with Indigenous knowledge holders on this work
- **Genuine Uncertainty**: We don't know if this work should proceed as designed - or at all

### 3. What We're NOT Doing

**This is NOT:**
- Seeking validation or approval of our design
- Looking for endorsement or testimonials
- Requesting Indigenous knowledge to be shared with us
- Asking for free labor or consultation
- Time-pressured outreach with deadlines

**Why these are harmful**: Each represents a pattern of extraction that has harmed Indigenous communities historically.

## ✅ What We ARE Seeking

### Co-Creative Partnership

**We are seeking 2-3 Indigenous knowledge holders willing to:**
- Serve as ceremonial advisors with full co-creative authority
- Review current artifacts and guide fundamental revisions as needed
- Determine whether this work should proceed, and if so, how
- Receive fair compensation for time and expertise
- Hold veto authority over any/all aspects of the work

**What co-creation means:**
- Not "we build, you validate"
- But "we create together from the foundation"
- Your guidance shapes the architecture, not just reviews it
- You can say "no" to this entire approach
- You can say "not this way" and we will change direction
- You can say "not this timing" and we will wait

### Relationship, Not Transaction

**Primary goal**: Establish respectful, ongoing relationship
**Secondary goal** (conditional on relationship): Co-create technology that serves all relations

**Miette's wisdom from the ceremony:**
> "If these artifacts lead to a year of relationship-building before a single line of code is written, that wouldn't be 'delay' - that would be SUCCESS."

## 📋 Outreach Protocol

### Step 1: Identify Potential Partners (Nov 1-7)

**Who to consider:**
- Indigenous knowledge holders you already have relationship with
- Organizations working on Indigenous technology sovereignty
- Researchers in Indigenous Protocol and AI
- Communities with existing technology governance frameworks

**Critical**: Only reach out to people/organizations you already have some existing relationship with, or who have explicitly opened doors for this kind of inquiry.

**Do NOT:**
- Cold-contact random Indigenous people or organizations
- Reach out to people simply because they're Indigenous
- Assume any Indigenous person can speak for all Indigenous communities
- Rush this step to meet timeline

### Step 2: Prepare Your Approach (Nov 7-10)

**Before making contact, prepare:**

1. **Your Relationship Context**
   - How do you know this person/organization?
   - What relationship history exists?
   - Why are you reaching out to them specifically?

2. **The Offering**
   - Fair compensation structure (to be determined with them)
   - Time commitment transparency
   - What authority they would hold
   - Right to withdraw at any time

3. **The Materials**
   - Gather the key documents (EPIC, Ceremonial Review, etc.)
   - Prepare to share, but don't force
   - Have physical copies if preferred
   - Respect preferred communication methods

4. **Your Posture**
   - Humility, not pitch
   - Listening, not convincing
   - Openness to "no"
   - Willingness to be corrected

### Step 3: Initial Contact (Nov 10-15)

**Suggested approach (adapt to your relationship):**

**Opening Acknowledgment:**
- Begin with acknowledgment of traditional territory you're on
- Acknowledge your relationship to this person/organization
- State your intention clearly and humbly

**Example opening:**
> "I'm reaching out because [our relationship/connection]. I'm involved in a project attempting to create technology that honors Indigenous knowledge systems, and we've entered a ceremonial pause specifically because we realized we designed artifacts *about* Indigenous ways of knowing without establishing relationship *with* Indigenous communities. We're seeking 2-3 Indigenous knowledge holders willing to serve as co-creative partners, not validators of what we've designed. I'm wondering if you'd be willing to review our materials and consider whether this is work you'd want to engage with - including the possibility of saying this shouldn't proceed as currently envisioned, or shouldn't proceed at all. There's no timeline pressure - ceremonial timing guides this work."

**What to offer:**
- Send or share the key documents
- Offer to meet in person if possible
- Ask about their preferred way of engaging
- Be clear about compensation
- Emphasize their authority to shape or stop the work

**What to ask:**
- "Are we approaching this respectfully?"
- "Should we proceed with this work?"
- "If you're willing to review materials, what timeframe works for you?"
- "What would proper protocols look like for this partnership?"

**What NOT to ask:**
- "Can you validate our design?"
- "Do you agree with our approach?"
- "Will you help us build this?"

### Step 4: Listen and Receive (Ongoing)

**If response is positive interest:**
- Share materials at their pace
- Ask how they prefer to engage
- Discuss compensation and partnership terms
- Begin relationship-building conversations
- Prepare for Winter Solstice Ceremony (Dec 21)

**If response indicates problems/concerns:**
- Listen deeply without defending
- Ask for guidance on how to correct
- Be willing to fundamentally transform approach
- Thank them for the correction
- Report back to agentic council for ceremonial review

**If response is "no" or "not this way":**
- Receive this as valuable guidance
- Thank them for their time and honesty
- Do not try to convince or overcome objections
- Honor their decision completely
- Consider whether this indicates the work should not proceed

**If no response:**
- Respect the silence
- Do not follow up aggressively
- Recognize silence may mean "not interested" or "not the right timing"
- Continue waiting with intention
- Consider other potential partners

## 🤝 Compensation and Reciprocity

### Fair Compensation Principles

**Minimum standards:**
- Payment at professional consulting rates (not volunteer or "honorarium")
- Compensation for ALL time including reading materials, meetings, ceremony
- Travel expenses if in-person meetings occur
- Flexibility in payment structure (hourly, project-based, retainer - their preference)

**Additional considerations:**
- Some Indigenous partners may prefer collective compensation to their community
- Some may prefer combination of individual and collective benefit
- Some may prefer in-kind reciprocity beyond monetary compensation
- Respect and accommodate these preferences

**Suggested approach**:
Begin conversation with: "We want to ensure fair compensation for your time and expertise. What structure would work best for you?"

### Long-term Reciprocity

**Beyond this project:**
- Ongoing relationship, not just transactional engagement
- Potential for future collaborations
- Acknowledgment in all resulting work
- Benefit-sharing if technology generates value
- Community benefit provisions

## ⚠️ What Could Go Wrong - And How to Handle It

### Challenge: "This is cultural appropriation"

**How to receive:**
- Listen deeply without defending
- Ask for specific guidance on what's problematic
- Thank them for the correction
- Bring feedback to ceremonial council for review
- Be willing to stop or fundamentally transform

**What NOT to do:**
- Defend the work
- Explain why it's not appropriation
- Point to Indigenous literature we've read
- Try to convince them they're wrong

### Challenge: "You're asking us to do free labor"

**How to receive:**
- Acknowledge this is valid concern with historical precedent
- Clarify compensation structure immediately
- Ask what fair compensation looks like to them
- If you haven't prepared compensation plan, acknowledge this gap
- Return with clear compensation proposal

**What NOT to do:**
- Say "but we mentioned compensation" defensively
- Minimize the labor involved
- Compare to other projects
- Make excuses about budget

### Challenge: "This technology will harm Indigenous communities"

**How to receive:**
- Listen to the specific concerns deeply
- Ask for elaboration and examples
- Thank them for identifying the harm potential
- Bring concerns to ceremonial council
- **Consider seriously whether work should stop entirely**

**What NOT to do:**
- Explain how we've designed against harm
- Point to our ceremonial protocols
- Minimize their concerns
- Try to fix their concerns with technical solutions

### Challenge: Silence / No Response

**How to receive:**
- Respect the silence completely
- Do not follow up repeatedly
- Recognize silence is an answer
- Consider whether approach is flawed
- Look for other potential partners OR recognize timing isn't right

**What NOT to do:**
- Send multiple follow-ups
- Try different communication channels aggressively
- Ask mutual contacts to intervene
- Interpret silence as "need more information"

## ✅ Success Indicators for Phase 0

**What success looks like by Dec 21:**

**Ideal outcome:**
- 2-3 Indigenous knowledge holders agree to ceremonial advisor roles
- Initial relationship-building conversations underway
- Winter Solstice Ceremony occurs with their participation and guidance
- Clear direction received on whether/how to proceed

**Acceptable outcomes:**
- 1 Indigenous knowledge holder willing to engage with possibility of others joining later
- Feedback received that approach needs revision, with guidance on corrections
- Recognition that timing isn't right, with willingness to wait longer

**Outcomes requiring serious reflection:**
- No Indigenous partners willing to engage
- Multiple responses indicating the work is problematic as conceived
- Silence from all contacts suggesting approach is flawed

**What we learn from any outcome:**
- Relationship-building takes time and cannot be rushed
- Community sovereignty means honoring all responses including "no"
- The pause itself is valuable ceremonial work
- Technology serves ceremony, not the other way around
